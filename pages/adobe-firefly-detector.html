How Adobe Firefly Creates Images — The Visual Tells

Adobe Firefly is trained on licensed stock imagery, which is why its outputs look unnaturally polished. It's a strength that becomes a weakness once you know where to look.

Skin texture is the first tell. Firefly smooths skin far too evenly. Real skin has pores, fine hairs, and micro-variations in tone. Firefly gives you porcelain. Look at the cheekbones, jawline, and neck — if the texture feels airbrushed to perfection, that's Firefly's signature.

Hair strands follow patterns too rigid. Real hair has chaos: flyaways, uneven texture, shadows that don't align perfectly. Firefly renders hair with algorithmic consistency. Each strand group flows in mathematically smooth curves. Watch the edges where hair meets the background — Firefly often creates halos or soft blur artifacts that real photos don't have.

Eye reflections are too symmetrical. Firefly places specular highlights on both eyes at identical angles and intensities. Real lighting doesn't work that way. One eye catches more light than the other. The pupils might reflect a window, a phone, or a lamp — but Firefly tends toward blank uniformity. Stare at the eyes. That's where it falls apart.

Backgrounds blend seamlessly because they're not real. Firefly doesn't photograph backgrounds; it generates them. Walls, trees, and bokeh are mathematically interpolated. This means depth-of-field blur is often inconsistent. Objects that should be out of focus stay sharp. Edges between foreground and background lack the natural texture noise that real cameras capture.

Lighting comes from nowhere and everywhere. Real faces have shadows cast by ambient light, key light, and fill light sources. Firefly flattens this. You see evenly lit faces without hard shadows on the neck or jaw. The light seems to come from the image itself, not from a physical source.
