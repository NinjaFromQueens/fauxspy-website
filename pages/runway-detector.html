The tells in Runway AI video images — where the illusion breaks

Runway AI video is good at mimicking real cameras. The skin tones look warm. Hair flows naturally. But the generator leaves fingerprints, and once you know where to look, you'll spot them.

The first tell is hands and fingers. Runway AI video often softens or blurs fingers, or renders them with subtle width inconsistencies. Real photos show defined cuticles, nail beds, and joint lines. Look at the hands first.

Backgrounds are the second weak point. While Runway AI video excels at faces, backgrounds often have spatial confusion. Walls don't quite align. Shadows don't land where light sources suggest they should. Trees or architectural lines bend unnaturally. Your eye catches something off, but you can't name it—that's the hallmark of AI.

Hair is harder to fake than people think. Runway AI video can generate realistic-looking hair, but close inspection reveals texture flatness. Individual strands don't catch light the way real hair does. Flyaways disappear. The hair looks airbrushed, almost painted.

Lighting inconsistencies appear when multiple light sources should be visible. If someone is standing in front of a window with warm interior lighting, Runway AI video sometimes renders conflicting shadow directions. The left side of the face might be lit as if the light is on the right. Faux Spy catches these spatial contradictions that the human brain flags as "off" but can't articulate.
