Online dating: frequently asked questions

How many people use online dating apps?

Approximately 323 million people use online dating apps globally as of 2024 (Statista). In the United States, 30% of adults have used a dating app or website — rising to 53% of adults under 30 (Pew Research 2023). Approximately 40% of US couples who formed relationships in recent years met online (Stanford Social Dynamics Lab 2024).

What percentage of dating app profiles are fake?

1 in 4 online dating profiles is estimated to be fake or fraudulent, according to McAfee's 2026 Modern Love Report. Individual platforms report massive removal numbers: Bumble removes ~900,000 fake profiles per month, Tinder removed 5.8 million policy-violating profiles in H1 2024, and Facebook removes over 1 billion fake accounts quarterly across its platform.

How common is online dating fraud?

The FTC reported $1.14 billion in romance fraud losses in 2023. The FBI IC3 reported $672 million in romance/confidence fraud in 2024. 52% of online daters have encountered a suspected catfish (Pew 2022). 13% have been asked for money by someone they met on a dating app (Pew 2023). The true total — including unreported cases — is estimated at $4.7 billion annually.

Which dating app has the most fake profiles?

Plenty of Fish (POF) accounts for 78% of all fake dating app installations (McAfee 2026). However, by raw removal volume, Tinder (5.8M removed H1 2024) and Bumble (~900K/month) lead. Facebook Dating inherits fake accounts from the broader Facebook platform, which is the most common starting point for scams — 57% of FTC scam origin reports trace to Facebook.

Is online dating safe?

Online dating is widely used and millions form genuine relationships through it, but significant fraud risks exist. Key protective steps: check profile photos with an AI detector (Faux Spy is free); run a reverse image search; request a real-time selfie; insist on a live video call before meeting; always meet in public for the first time; and never send money to someone you've only met online.

What is the fastest-growing online dating scam?

Pig butchering (sha zhu pan) is the fastest-growing variant. The scammer builds a romantic connection, then introduces a fake cryptocurrency investment platform. The victim makes small "test" investments that show fake profits, then invests a large amount — which the scammer steals. Global pig butchering losses are estimated at $12.4 billion in 2024. The FBI has designated it a top financial crime priority.

How has AI changed online dating fraud?

AI has made fake profiles dramatically harder to detect. Romance scammers now use AI image generators to create realistic fake photos of people who don't exist — faces with no match online that reverse image search cannot find. 1 in 4 Americans encountered an AI-generated dating app photo in 2026. AI chatbots also allow scammers to manage dozens of fake relationships simultaneously. AI detection tools are now essential for online dating safety.

What should I do if I suspect I'm being scammed on a dating app?

Stop contact immediately. Right-click their photo and check it with Faux Spy — if it's AI-generated, you're talking to a scammer. Run a reverse image search. Demand a live video call. If they've already asked for money: do not pay; report to IC3.gov and ReportFraud.ftc.gov; contact your bank immediately if money was sent; and report the profile on the platform. The AARP Fraud Watch Network Helpline (1-877-908-3360) provides free support.
