What each tool does — and where each one fails

PimEyes is one of the most powerful facial recognition search engines available to the public. Upload a face, and it searches a massive database of indexed web images to find where that face has appeared — other social profiles, news sites, adult content sites, anywhere public. It's used for privacy monitoring, catfish verification, and tracking where your own face is being used without consent.

PimEyes's limitation is the same as every face search tool: it can only find a face if that face already exists online. An AI-generated profile photo was just created. It has never been indexed. PimEyes searches and finds nothing — which tells you nothing useful.

Faux Spy doesn't search the internet at all. It analyzes the pixel-level mathematical structure of the image. AI generators like Midjourney and DALL-E leave statistical artifacts in images that cameras don't produce — subtle patterns in pixel distribution, frequency-domain signatures from the generation process. Faux Spy looks for those signatures and returns a verdict: AI Photo or Real Photo, with a confidence score.

The complementary use case: run both

If you're trying to verify whether someone on a dating app is who they claim to be, the most thorough check combines both tools — in this order:

  1. Check with Faux Spy first. Hover over the profile photo in Chrome, click Investigate. If it comes back as AI Photo with high confidence, you're done — the face was generated, not photographed. Skip PimEyes.
  2. If Faux Spy says Real Photo, run PimEyes. Upload the photo to PimEyes to check whether the face appears elsewhere online. If results come back showing the photo on a different profile under a different name — stolen photo confirmed.
  3. No result on either tool isn't a guarantee. A clean Faux Spy result means the image's structure looks like a real photograph. A clean PimEyes result means the face hasn't appeared in PimEyes's indexed database. Neither is certainty — they're signals.

This two-step approach covers the full attack surface: AI-generated faces (Faux Spy) and stolen real photos (PimEyes).

Why PimEyes alone isn't enough anymore

PimEyes was built for an era when fake profile photos were stolen from real people — copy a photo off Instagram, paste it into a dating app, done. Reverse face search exposed that immediately: find where the face appears elsewhere, catch the scammer.

AI image generation broke this model. A scammer who uses Midjourney takes 30 seconds to generate a convincing face that has never appeared anywhere online. PimEyes searches its entire database and returns nothing. The scammer is invisible to the tool.

AI-generated profile photos are now common enough to have their own statistic: 35% of Americans have spotted AI-generated or AI-modified photos on dating apps (McAfee, 2026). The problem has scaled past what reverse search alone can handle.
