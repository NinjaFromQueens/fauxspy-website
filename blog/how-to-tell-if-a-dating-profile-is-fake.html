How to Tell If a Dating Profile Is Fake

The old advice — "reverse image search the photos" — stopped working in 2023. Here's what actually catches fakes now.

A guy I know matched with someone on Hinge in February. Beautiful photos. Pediatric nurse in Charlotte. Two weeks of texting, voice notes that felt warm, even a couple of FaceTime calls that "kept cutting out." Then came the soft pitch about a crypto account her uncle helped her set up. He didn't send money. He came close.

The profile was a composite. AI face on the main photo, stolen Instagram shots for the candids, a script for the conversation. He only figured it out because one of the "candid" photos had a Starbucks cup with Mandarin characters on it — and she'd told him she'd never left the East Coast.

That's where we are now. Fake profiles aren't sloppy anymore. The FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center logged $672,009,052 in romance scam losses across 17,910 complaints in 2024, and the FTC put broader romance fraud losses at $1.14 billion across 64,003 reports. The median victim, per the FTC, loses $2,000. The average, per the FBI's narrower dataset, is $37,521.

The signs are still there. You just have to know where to look.

One polished photo is more suspicious than five mediocre ones

Most people think a fake profile means bad photos. It's the opposite. Real people have a phone full of garbage — bad lighting, a double chin, a shot from 2019 with an ex cropped out badly. They pick four or five they like and roll with it.

Fake profiles tend to have too good a photo set. One stunning headshot. Or three photos that all look like they're from the same 20-minute session. Or — and this is the new tell — a mix of one AI-generated face shot and several stolen lifestyle photos where the face is conveniently turned, blurred, or partially covered.

If every photo shows the person at the same weight, same hair, same approximate angle, that's a flag. Real life leaves more mess than that.

Open the photos full-screen. Look at the earrings, the collarbone, the background. AI still botches asymmetric jewelry, melts text on signs, and turns the hair behind the ear into a vaguely-shaped blob.

The bio reads like a Mad Libs entry

Real bios are weird. They reference a specific bar, a niche show, a job nobody's heard of. They use lowercase when they shouldn't and have one typo the person didn't notice.

Fake bios sound like they were written by someone trying to appeal to the maximum number of people. "Love to travel, love to laugh, looking for my partner in crime. Equally happy in heels or hiking boots." That's not a person. That's a template.

Search the bio text in Google with quotes around it. If the exact phrasing shows up on five other profiles or on a "best Tinder bio examples" listicle, you have your answer.

Same with the job. "Engineer" with no company. "Doctor, currently on deployment." "Oil rig worker." Those last two are romance scam classics — they explain away the inability to meet in person and the inability to take a normal call.

They want to leave the app within 48 hours

This is the single most reliable behavioral signal. A real match might suggest moving to text after a few days of conversation. A scammer is itching to get you onto WhatsApp, Telegram, or Google Chat by message three.

Why? Because dating apps run their own fraud detection. Hinge, Bumble, and Tinder all flag accounts based on message patterns, IP addresses, and reports. Once you're off-platform, the scammer is operating in the dark and can use a phone number that traces back nowhere.

If they push to leave the app fast, slow down. A real person waiting a week to swap numbers is normal. A "nurse working a 36-hour shift" who needs to give you her Telegram in the first hour is not.

The "333 rule" everyone asks about — and why it doesn't help here

People keep searching for the "333 rule in dating apps." For the curious: it's an informal idea that you should match with three people, message three at a time, and plan three dates per week to avoid burnout. It's a sanity rule, not a safety rule.

It won't tell you if a profile is fake. But it accidentally helps, because scammers rely on you being emotionally focused on them alone. If you're juggling normal conversations with three real people, the one who's love-bombing you on day four starts to look very weird very fast.

That contrast is the point. Fake profiles count on isolation.

Ask for something specific and unfakeable

Forget the old advice about asking for a video call. Real-time deepfake video is now within reach of a motivated scammer with a consumer GPU — biometric fraud surged 704% in recent reporting cycles, and "the video kept freezing" is the new "my camera is broken."

Ask for something a deepfake can't easily fake on the fly. A photo of them holding up three fingers in the next ten minutes. A photo with a handwritten note that includes your name and a random word you pick. A voice note saying a specific phrase.

Real people send these in a couple minutes and think nothing of it. Scammers stall, get offended that you don't trust them, or send something that doesn't quite match what you asked for.

One Reddit thread on r/OnlineDating documented a tactic where scammers occasionally hide a word in a profile photo and then ask you to email them with that word as the subject line — a sorting mechanism for incoming marks. If you see odd text overlaid on a photo and a prompt to contact them off-platform, close the tab.

Reverse image search still works — just not the way you think

Reverse image search used to catch scammers using stolen photos. It still works for that. But it can't catch AI-generated faces, because those faces don't exist anywhere else on the internet. A Google Images result of "no matches found" is no longer reassuring. It's neutral.

What you actually want is an AI-detection check on the image itself — not "has this image been posted somewhere else" but "was this image generated by a model in the first place."

The conversation itself has tells

Scammer conversation patterns are remarkably consistent once you've seen a few. They reply fast, but their replies don't quite match what you said. They ask questions that feel like a survey — what do you do, where do you live, are you married, do you own your home. They escalate emotional intimacy on a timeline that doesn't match how humans actually fall for each other.

And the grammar slips. Not always. But watch for native-English mistakes that aren't typos — "I am very happy for chat with you," "kindly send your number," weirdly formal sign-offs. Many romance scam operations run out of call-center-style buildings in West Africa and Southeast Asia, working from scripts in their second or third language.

Inconsistencies pile up if you let them. They said they were 34 last week and 36 today. They said they have a sister, then mention being an only child. Keep a mental log. Real people don't trip over their own biography.

What to do when you're 80% sure

You don't need to confront anyone. You don't owe them an exit interview. Unmatch, block, and report the profile through the app's reporting tool. Hinge, Tinder, and Bumble all have a "fake profile" or "scam" category specifically.

If money or personal information already changed hands, file with IC3.gov and the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov. Those reports feed the statistics that get law enforcement budget and feed back into platform detection systems. Underreporting is one reason this problem keeps growing — many victims don't realize they've been scammed for months, and by then the money is gone.

The goal isn't to become paranoid about everyone you match with. Most profiles are real. The goal is to know what fake looks like so the moment one shows up, your gut goes off before your heart does.

Frequently Asked Questions

How to check if a dating profile is real?

Ask for a video call early on—fake profiles usually can't accommodate. Check if their photos appear in reverse image searches (Google Images) or on other websites. Real profiles typically have multiple photos from different times and settings.

What is the 333 rule in dating apps?

The 333 rule suggests: chat for 3 days, talk on phone for 3 weeks, then meet in person after 3 months. It's designed to slow down romance-scammers who push for quick meetings or money requests.

How to verify a dating profile?

Use reverse image search on their photos, request a live video call with them holding a sign with their username, and check for consistency between their stories and social media. Legitimate users are usually willing to verify their identity.

What are red flags for fake profiles?

Watch for: suspiciously perfect photos, vague/generic bios, quick declarations of love, requests for money or gift cards, unwillingness to video call, and stories that constantly change. They often move conversations off the app immediately or push for personal information.
