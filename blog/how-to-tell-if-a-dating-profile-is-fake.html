How to Tell If a Dating Profile Is Fake

Reverse image search used to catch most fakes. Then AI-generated faces happened, and the old advice stopped working.

A woman in Ohio sent $47,000 to a man she'd been talking to on Hinge for four months. He had six photos. He answered her video call requests with excuses about his work schedule on an oil rig. He was never real. The face on the profile didn't exist anywhere on the internet because it had been generated the morning the account was created.

That's the new shape of this problem. The FTC reported $1.14 billion in romance scam losses across 64,003 cases in 2024. The FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center logged $672,009,052 in losses across 17,910 complaints in the same year. Biometric fraud — which includes AI-generated faces and deepfake video — surged 704% according to industry reporting.

The old playbook for spotting fake profiles assumed scammers were stealing photos from real people. That assumption is now wrong about half the time.

Reverse image search catches the lazy ones, not the dangerous ones

For years, the standard advice was: drop the profile photo into Google Images or TinEye. If it shows up on a Russian modeling site or attached to a different name, it's fake. That advice still works — for scammers using stolen photos.

But an AI-generated face doesn't exist anywhere else. There's nothing to find. The image was created by a diffusion model thirty seconds before the profile went live, and it will never appear on another website unless the scammer puts it there. Reverse image search returns nothing. That blank result is not proof the person is real. It might be the opposite.

Most people read a clean reverse image search as a green light. They're wrong. On Tinder, Bumble, and Hinge in 2026, a face that returns zero results is just as likely to be synthetic as authentic.

The tells are in the photos themselves, not the metadata

AI image generators have gotten frighteningly good at faces. They're still bad at the stuff around the face. Look at the edges of the photo, not the center.

Earrings that don't match. One earring is a stud, the other is a hoop. Glasses with frames that change thickness between the left and right side. Necklaces that vanish into a collarbone and reappear at a different angle. Background text that's almost-letters but not quite — signs in restaurants that read like a foreign alphabet someone made up.

Hands are the classic giveaway, but the tell has shifted. Modern generators usually get five fingers right. What they still botch: how the hand holds something. A coffee cup floating in a fingertip pinch. A dog leash that doesn't actually connect to the collar. A phone gripped from a physically impossible angle.

And teeth. Look at the teeth. AI-generated smiles tend to have either too-perfect symmetry or weird asymmetric merging where two teeth blur into one. Real teeth have small imperfections — a slight overlap, a yellowed canine, a tiny gap. Generators average them out.

One great photo is more suspicious than five mediocre ones

Real people have bad photos. They have a photo from their cousin's wedding where the lighting is off. A gym selfie with a weird angle. A picture with a friend cropped out badly. A blurry one from a concert.

A profile with six photos that all look like portfolio shots — same skin tone rendering, same general framing, same studio-grade lighting — is a problem. Either you're talking to a model (unlikely) or you're looking at six outputs from the same prompt with minor variations.

Check for photographic continuity. Does the person appear in different locations across years? Are there photos with other identifiable humans whose faces aren't conveniently turned away? Is there a single photo that contains a verifiable real-world element — a sports team jersey from a specific game, a concert wristband, a recognizable city skyline at a specific time of day?

Scammers using AI rarely bother with this kind of texture. It's hard to generate consistently. The same face across six different "real" environments still trips up most models.

The bio gives them up faster than the photos

One pattern keeps showing up: thin bios. Profiles with almost no information, or with generic statements that could apply to anyone, are statistically more likely to be fake. "I love to travel and try new foods" is not a personality. It's a template.

Real profiles contain specifics that would be useless to a scammer. The name of a small-town high school. A weirdly specific opinion about a regional sandwich. A reference to a job title that requires explanation. Scammers avoid specifics because specifics create surface area for contradiction later.

Also watch for inconsistencies inside the profile itself. A bio that says they live in Denver but their photos all have Florida palm trees in the background. A claimed age of 32 with a job history that would require them to be 45. An education listed at a university that doesn't offer the major they mention.

The classic scammer-screening trick still works on some lower-effort operators: ask them to put a specific word in their next message, or take a photo holding up three fingers. The pros won't do it. The amateurs will tell you their phone camera is broken.

Conversation patterns reveal what photos hide

The FBI confirms that romance scammers are increasingly using AI for both faces and text. ChatGPT-style language models now write the messages. That means the old tells — broken English, weird phrasing, copy-pasted love poems — are largely gone.

What's left is structural. Scammers move fast on emotion and slow on logistics. Within two weeks they'll be calling you their soulmate. They will not, however, agree to a spontaneous video call. They will not meet for coffee even if you live in the same city. There will always be a reason — military deployment, an oil rig contract, a sick parent in another country, a work trip to Dubai that keeps extending.

Real people who want to date you eventually want to see you. Fake ones invent infinite reasons why that's six weeks away. Then ten. Then never.

And the money question always comes. Sometimes it's a direct ask. More often it's a "crypto investment opportunity" or a request to help cover a shipping fee for a gift they're sending you. The median victim loses $2,000 according to FTC data. The FBI's higher-loss dataset puts the average at $37,521. Most victims send money more than once before they stop.

What to actually do when something feels off

Trust the feeling. The single most consistent thing victims report after the fact is that something felt wrong before they sent money. They overrode the feeling because the relationship felt real. The feeling was right.

Concrete steps that work in 2026:

Run the photos through an AI detector, not just reverse image search. If the face was generated by Stable Diffusion, Midjourney, or one of the face-specific models, a detector built for that will flag it. Reverse image search will not.

Ask for a live video call with a specific request — hold up two fingers, tilt your head to the left. Deepfake video is real, but real-time deepfakes that respond to arbitrary instructions are still hard. Scammers will refuse, postpone, or claim technical problems.

Search the exact phrasing of suspicious messages. Romance scammers often reuse scripts across dozens of targets. A unique-sounding sentence pasted into Google sometimes returns r/Scams threads from other victims who got the same message word-for-word.

Never send money. Not for a plane ticket. Not for customs fees. Not for a medical emergency. Not for a "guaranteed" crypto investment. There is no scenario where a person you have not met in physical reality needs your money for a legitimate reason.

Frequently Asked Questions

How to check if a dating profile is real?

Ask for a video call early on—scammers usually refuse. Reverse image search their photos on Google Images; fake profiles often use stolen pictures. Check if their story is consistent and they're willing to verify themselves.

What are the signs of a dating scammer?

They move quickly to profess feelings, ask for money, or want to chat off the dating app immediately. They'll have excuses for why they can't video call or meet in person. Their backstory often involves being abroad, deployed military, or having a sudden emergency.

What are red flags for fake profiles?

Photos that look professionally modeled, vague or generic bios, and profiles with only 1-2 pictures. They avoid answering personal questions, push conversations toward money or off-platform quickly, or have inconsistencies in their story.

What does a fake profile look like?

Usually has 1-3 extremely polished or stock-photo-quality images, minimal or overly generic bio, and little engagement history. The profile feels rushed or incomplete, with spelling errors or awkward phrasing, especially if claiming to be a native English speaker.
