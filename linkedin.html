LinkedIn's fake profile problem is getting worse

LinkedIn removed over 95 million fake accounts in 2023. That number keeps climbing because the tool to create a convincing fake profile — including a photorealistic headshot that doesn't belong to any real person — now takes less than a minute.

The fakes aren't crude anymore. A generated headshot looks like a professional photo: good lighting, neutral background, the kind of image you'd expect on a real profile. They specifically don't look suspicious. That's the point.

Who uses fake LinkedIn profiles

Fake recruiters — one of the fastest-growing scams. A convincing recruiter profile messages you about a job opportunity, builds rapport over weeks, then pivots to asking for personal information or advance fees. The profile photo is AI-generated. The recruiter doesn't exist.

Romance catfishing via LinkedIn — LinkedIn's professional context makes people lower their guard. The "successful professional" messaging you isn't who they claim to be, and their photo was generated by an AI.

Phantom candidates — AI-generated profiles are used to flood hiring pipelines, probe company systems, or gather intel on job postings and compensation. A fake applicant with an AI headshot is harder to catch than an obvious bot account.

Competitor intelligence — fake employees from competing companies, fake industry analysts, fake journalists. All designed to get accepted and gather information.

What to do if something feels off

Faux Spy covers AI-generated images. For a complete check, pair it with a reverse image search — paste the photo into Google Images or TinEye to see if it appears elsewhere on the web. Real people usually have an online footprint; AI-generated personas usually don't.

Other red flags alongside a suspicious headshot: profiles created recently, no mutual connections, messages that escalate unusually fast, job offers that seem too good, or requests to move conversation off LinkedIn quickly.

Common questions

Does it work on the LinkedIn mobile app?

Faux Spy works on LinkedIn's browser version at linkedin.com in Chrome. The iOS and Android apps can't run browser extensions. If you're suspicious about a profile on mobile, open the profile URL in Chrome on your desktop to check.

Can it catch photos stolen from real people's profiles?

No — Faux Spy detects AI-generated images specifically. For stolen real photos, use Google Images or TinEye reverse image search. The two tools work well together: Faux Spy catches synthetic faces, reverse search catches stolen real ones.

What should I do if I find a fake profile?

Report it to LinkedIn via the "More" menu on the profile page → Report/Block → Fake profile. LinkedIn takes these reports seriously and acts on them. If a fake recruiter has already contacted you and you've shared personal information, consider reporting to the FTC as well.
